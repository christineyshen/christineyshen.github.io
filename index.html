Introduction

With annual inflation rate at 2.0% (https://www.thebalance.com/u-s-inflation-rate-history-by-year-and-forecast-3306093) and unemployment below 4.0% (https://www.npr.org/2018/10/05/654417887/u-s-unemployment-rate-drops-to-3-7-percent-lowest-in-nearly-50-years), it appears that America's job market has continued to improve since the 2008 financial crisis, although cost of living has also increased since the past century.

Although Maryland is the richest state in America and Washington, D.C. has a very high cost of living, I suspect there are hidden trends in income and occupation and hidden relationships between these two variables that contribute to the socioeconomic stratification in the Maryland-D.C. metropolitan region. Furthermore, while some Millennials have excellent job prospects, others have to study additional degrees just to pay the bills and feed their families. This project explores this.

I used the May 2017 State Occupational Employment and Wage Estimates for Maryland and the District of Columbia from the the Bureau of Labor Statistics (https://www.bls.gov/oes/special.requests/oesm17st.zip). I chose these two areas because they lie in the same metropolitan area and they are familiar to me. I turned both files into CSVs and deleted extraneous columns, such as area, extra state columns, occupational group, and true/false columns for annual and hourly wage.

Conclusion

In conclusion, Maryland appears to hire more people compared to Washington, D.C. However, Washington, D.C. offers a higher wage to workers at all levels, compared to Maryland. In both areas, there is less deviation between the 10th and 50th percentiles for hourly and annual wage, compared to between the 50th and 90th percentiles. Also, higher-paying jobs can earn much more in Washington, D.C., compared to Maryland, but lower-paying jobs would not experience a similar difference in earnings.

It is interesting that both Office and Administrative Support, and Food Preparing and Serving rank among the top four occupations employing the most people in both Maryland and Washington, D.C. Perhaps this is due to the high demand for culinary experiences, catering, and tourism, as well as administrative work in both areas. In Maryland, Sales and Related Occupations, and Healthcare Practitioners and Technicians are also in the top four employers. In Washington, D.C., Business and Financial is the largest employer, and Management is also in the top four.

There appears to be little to no correlation between total employment and mean annual wage for all occupations in Maryland. However, in Washington, D.C., there appears to be a positive correlation.

Overall, people looking for higher wages should look towards Washington, D.C., regardless of the specific job's payscale. However, those who are in high-paying jobs will earn significantly more in Washington, D.C. than in Maryland, compared to those in lower-paying jobs. Those working in healthcare and sales will likely find more opportunity in Maryland, while those in management and business will find more opportunity in Washington, D.C. I would suggest running analyses on the other states in the United States before reaching a conclusion about the correlation between total employment and mean annual wage.
